NTIIssueBrief
=============

The end of the Cold War and the dissolution of the Soviet Union changed the geopolitical landscape dramatically. The specter of a conflict between the two superpowers, inevitably escalating into a full-scale nuclear war, has faded away. More than two decades since the end of the Cold War, Russia and the U.S. still maintain all three legs of their strategic nuclear “triad” — heavy bombers, ballistic missile submarines (SSBN) and land-based intercontinental ballistic missiles (ICBM). Concurrently, a significant debate has arisen within the United States as to whether it needs three methods of deploying its nuclear weapons. In the Russian Federation this debate is not taking place; rather than debating whether it needs all three legs of its nuclear triad, Russia is recapitalizing and modernizing its entire strategic arsenal. What do these actions demonstrate? What does this say about the deterrent value of the triad in Russia? As tensions between the Russia and the U.S. rise and as both countries modernize their nuclear arsenals, it is necessary to reexamine nuclear policies in Russia in order to unravel preconceived notions and clear up misconceptions.
